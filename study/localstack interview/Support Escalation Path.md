# Support Escalation Path

## Concept

An escalation path defines how an issue moves from "customer reported it" to "resolved," and who owns it at each stage. A TAM needs to know this cold — both to set correct customer expectations and to route issues efficiently internally.

**Typical structure:**
- **Tier 1 / initial triage** — often Support Engineering, handling the first response and diagnosing whether an issue is configuration, environment, or a genuine product bug/parity gap
- **TAM involvement** — for accounts with a dedicated TAM, the TAM is often looped in for anything with account-health implications (recurring issue, blocking a key workflow, risk to renewal) even if the technical resolution itself is owned by Support/Engineering
- **Engineering escalation** — genuine bugs or parity gaps that need a code-level fix get routed to the relevant engineering team, usually requiring a clear reproduction case
- **SLA differences by segment** — Enterprise accounts typically have contractually defined response/resolution SLAs; self-serve/Hobby accounts are generally supported through documentation and community channels without formal SLAs

**The TAM's specific role in escalation:**
- **Coordinator, not resolver** — a TAM doesn't typically debug the emulator themselves; they make sure the right people are engaged, the customer is kept informed, and the business context (why this matters, how urgent) is communicated clearly to engineering
- **Expectation-setting** — being honest about what's known vs. unknown, and avoiding overpromising a fix timeline, is one of the most common ways TAMs either build or erode trust during an escalation
- **Closing the loop** — following up after resolution to confirm the fix actually addressed the customer's need, not just that a ticket was closed

**Why this matters for a TAM:** escalations are high-visibility, high-trust moments. Handling them well — clear communication, appropriate urgency, honest expectation-setting — is one of the fastest ways to build credibility with a technical customer base.

## Knowledge Checklist

- [ ] Can describe the typical tiers of an escalation path (Support → TAM involvement → Engineering)
- [ ] Understands the TAM's role as coordinator rather than technical resolver
- [ ] Knows SLA expectations typically differ by customer segment (Enterprise vs. self-serve)
- [ ] Can explain what a "clear reproduction case" means and why engineering needs it
- [ ] Understands the risk of overpromising a fix timeline to a customer
- [ ] Knows to close the loop with a customer after resolution, not just after ticket closure
