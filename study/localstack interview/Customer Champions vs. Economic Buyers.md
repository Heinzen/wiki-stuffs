# Customer Champions vs. Economic Buyers

## Concept

In most LocalStack accounts, the person who *uses* the product day-to-day is not the same person who *pays* for it — understanding this split, and managing both relationships, is a core TAM skill.

**The two roles:**
- **Champion (daily user)** — typically an individual engineer, tech lead, or platform team member who actually runs LocalStack, integrates it into CI/CD, and experiences its value (or friction) firsthand. This person's satisfaction drives grassroots trust and internal advocacy for the tool.
- **Economic buyer** — typically a platform lead, engineering manager, or procurement/budget owner who controls the contract decision: whether to renew, upgrade tiers, or expand seats. This person may have limited hands-on product experience and cares primarily about business outcomes (cost, risk, team productivity) rather than technical nuance.

**Why the split matters:**
- **Different conversations for different people** — a champion wants technical depth, roadmap visibility, and responsive support; an economic buyer wants ROI justification, predictable costs, and confidence the tool reduces risk rather than adding it
- **Fragility of single-champion accounts** — if the champion is the *only* internal advocate and they leave the company or change roles, the account's internal support can collapse even if the product itself is working well. This is a recognized churn risk pattern.
- **Champions surface expansion signals; buyers approve them** — a champion might be the first to say "we need a higher tier for this," but the TAM (often working with an Account Manager) needs to translate that into a business case the economic buyer will approve
- **QBR/EBR structuring** — this is part of why QBRs (more technical, champion-facing) and EBRs (more strategic, buyer/leadership-facing) exist as distinct cadences rather than one-size-fits-all meetings

**Why this matters for a TAM:** effective account management means deliberately cultivating relationships with *both* roles, not just the person who happens to email support the most — and proactively identifying when an account has dangerously thin champion coverage.

## Knowledge Checklist

- [ ] Can define the champion and economic buyer roles and how they typically differ
- [ ] Understands why single-champion accounts are a recognized churn risk
- [ ] Can explain how a technical signal from a champion gets translated into a business case for an economic buyer
- [ ] Understands why QBRs and EBRs are pitched at different audiences (champion vs. buyer/leadership)
- [ ] Can identify signs that an account has thin or fragile champion coverage
- [ ] Recognizes the need to deliberately build relationships with both roles, not just the most vocal contact
