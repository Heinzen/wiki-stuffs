# Internal Stakeholders

## Concept

A TAM operates at the center of several internal teams, and knowing who owns what is essential for routing issues efficiently and representing the customer's needs to the right people.

**Key internal teams and their scope:**
- **Support Engineering** — owns first-line technical troubleshooting, ticket triage, and diagnosing whether an issue is configuration, environment, or a genuine bug/parity gap
- **Product** — owns roadmap prioritization, deciding which parity gaps or feature requests get addressed and when; the destination for well-aggregated Voice of Customer input
- **Solutions Engineering / Architecture** (where distinct from TAM) — may own deeper pre-sale or complex technical design conversations, especially for large or unusual customer architectures
- **Sales / Account Management** — owns the commercial relationship: pricing, contract terms, renewal/expansion negotiation; the TAM's closest collaborator on account-health-to-commercial-outcome translation
- **Engineering** — owns actual code-level fixes for confirmed bugs or parity gaps that Support/TAM have triaged and escalated with a clear reproduction case

**Why knowing this matters:**
- **Efficient routing** — knowing exactly which team owns a given type of problem avoids the common failure mode of over-escalating (sending simple config issues straight to engineering) or under-escalating (sitting on a genuine bug without looping in the right team)
- **Cross-functional advocacy** — a TAM's effectiveness depends heavily on relationships with these internal teams; being someone Product trusts to bring well-triaged feedback, or someone Support trusts to represent account context accurately, compounds over time
- **Avoiding duplicated effort** — in orgs where Solutions Engineering and TAM roles overlap somewhat, understanding the boundary prevents redundant customer-facing motions

**Why this matters for a TAM:** the role is fundamentally cross-functional — your effectiveness is often determined less by your own technical depth and more by how well you can mobilize the right internal team quickly and credibly.

## Knowledge Checklist

- [ ] Can name the core internal teams a TAM regularly interacts with
- [ ] Understands what each team owns (Support, Product, Solutions Engineering, Sales/AM, Engineering)
- [ ] Can explain the risk of over-escalating vs. under-escalating an issue
- [ ] Understands why cross-functional trust (with Support, Product) compounds over time
- [ ] Can describe how a TAM avoids duplicating effort with an adjacent Solutions Engineering function
- [ ] Recognizes that TAM effectiveness depends heavily on internal relationship quality, not just technical skill
